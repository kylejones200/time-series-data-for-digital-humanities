### Time Series Data for Digital Humanities
Time drives history. In the digital humanities, time series analysis
uncovers trends, anomalies, and repeating patterns in historical data.

A time series is a sequence of data points collected over time. The
order of the data matters. Each observation depends on the ones before
it. Time series methods track long-term shifts, such as the rise of
"freedom" in literature from 1800 to 2000. Political language changes
from election to election. Researchers merge time series data with other
datasets, such as unemployment, interest rates, wars, or policy changes,
to add context.


Time series data appears in different forms. Some datasets are
structured, such as population counts, economic growth, or CO₂ levels.
Other data, such as language, sentiment, or cultural trends, require
processing before analysis. Word frequency in books, newspapers, or
movies changes over time and reveals deeper patterns in public
discourse. Sentiment analysis tracks whether discussions around a
concept, such as democracy, grow more positive or negative.

Some historical data is structured and ready for analysis. Other data
requires transformation before we can analyze it. Newspaper articles
contain valuable historical information but raw text does not work as a
time series. Analysts extract numerical features, such as word
frequency, sentiment, or topic categories, before studying trends over
time.

Visualization is the first step of analysis. Graphing data over time
shows trends, cycles, and anomalies. The frequency of "democracy" in
newspapers reveals long-term growth, spikes around major elections, or
fluctuations tied to historical events. Breaking a time series into
components clarifies its meaning. The trend shows whether something is
increasing or decreasing over time. Seasonality captures recurring
patterns, such as election cycles. Noise represents random variations
that do not follow a clear pattern.



Time series analysis provides a structured way to study historical
change. Researchers collect, clean, and process data to apply these
methods to books, newspapers, speeches, or cultural records. Tracking
word usage, sentiment, and thematic trends becomes possible with
structured datasets. The next step is drawing deeper insights from this
data.
